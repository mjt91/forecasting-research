# SBBTS: Schrödinger-Bass Bridge for Synthetic Financial Time Series

## Summary

Alouadi et al. (2026) introduce SBBTS — a generative framework for synthetic financial time series that jointly calibrates both drift and stochastic volatility, unlike prior Schrödinger Bridge methods (which fix one or the other). On S&P 500 data, synthetic series generated by SBBTS improve downstream forecasting when used for data augmentation, producing higher classification accuracy and better Sharpe ratios vs. real-data-only training. The practical punchline: even very short synthetic series can help when real data is scarce, noisy, or non-stationary.

## Key Findings

- **Joint drift + stochastic volatility calibration**: Prior Schrödinger Bridge methods for TS either fixed volatility or fixed drift. SBBTS handles both, which matters for financial series where both drift and vol evolve over time.
- **Conditional transport decomposition**: The tractable decomposition into conditional transport problems makes multi-step generation efficient.
- **Heston model validation**: SBBTS accurately recovers Heston model parameters (vol of vol, correlation, etc.) that prior methods miss.
- **Data augmentation payoff**: S&P 500 downstream classification and Sharpe improves with synthetic-augmented training — not just marginal improvement but consistent gains across metrics.
- **Foundation in Schrödinger-Bass formulation**: The Schrödinger-Bass problem (from optimal transport) finds the diffusion closest to a reference while hitting terminal distribution constraints. SBBTS extends this to sequential/time series.

## Techniques Applicable to Production Forecasting

- **Synthetic data augmentation for scarce regimes**: In finance, some regimes (high vol, crisis, low liquidity) are underrepresented in training data. SBBTS can generate realistic synthetic paths for these regimes to improve model robustness.
- **Backtesting under synthetic scenarios**: Beyond augmentation, SBBTS could generate alternative history scenarios for stress testing — each synthetic path is consistent with the observed marginals and autocorrelation structure.
- **Generative data augmentation pipeline**: The framework could be inserted as a preprocessing step before training any forecasting model — particularly useful for deep learning models (N-BEATS, PatchTST, etc.) that benefit from more data.
- **Uncertainty quantification**: Synthetic ensembles give you a sense of forecast uncertainty that's grounded in data-generating process assumptions, not just ensemble variance.

## My Notes

As someone skeptical of pure DL hype in finance: the interesting thing about SBBTS is that it's grounded in financial model assumptions (Heston, Schrödinger-Bass bridges) rather than pure deep learning. The fact that synthetic data augmentation improves Sharpe and classification accuracy is a strong signal, but I'd want to see this validated on more diverse financial series and against simpler baselines (bootstrapping, historical simulation with jitter) before adopting it.

The practical setup: you'd generate synthetic series conditioned on observed marginals (e.g., match the empirical distribution of returns at each horizon), then mix synthetic + real for training. The paper shows this helps even when the synthetic series are short.

Potential application in our workflow: for cold-start forecasting problems (new product, new market), generative augmentation could give us realistic synthetic training data. The cold-start paper (2604.20370) is related — CDLF tackles cold-start via conditional diffusion, SBBTS via Schrödinger-Bass. Worth comparing the two approaches.
